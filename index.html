VC/PE Fundamentals

What is Venture Capital?


Venture Capital (VC) is a form of financing provided by firms or funds to startup, early-stage, and emerging companies that have deemed to have high growth potential.

Key Characteristics:

  • High uncertainty tolerance
  • "2 out of 10" mindset - expect most investments to fail
  • Focus on disruptive industries
  • Technology preference

Internal Rate of Return

(IRR)


Definition: The discount rate at which the Net Present Value (NPV) of all cash flows equals zero.

Use Case: To evaluate the profitability of potential investments. It provides a single percentage rate that can be compared across different investments.

Formula: Put NPV = 0 and solve for discount rate.

Pros: Accounts for the time value of money, considers all cash flows.

Cons: Assumes intermediate cash flows are reinvested at the IRR itself, which may not be realistic.

Extended Internal Rate of Return (XIRR)


Definition: A more flexible version of IRR that allows for cash flows to occur at irregular intervals, rather than at fixed periodic intervals.

Use Case: Ideal for investments like venture capital and private equity where capital calls and distributions are not strictly annual, but occur on specific dates.

Pros: Accurately reflects irregular cash flow timings. More realistic for many real-world investments.

Cons: Requires specific dates for each cash flow.

Modified Internal Rate of Return (MIRR)


Definition: A modification of the IRR which uses different rates for financing and reinvesting cash flows.

Use Case: Provides a more realistic return measure by allowing the investor to specify a separate reinvestment rate, often the cost of capital.

Formula: MIRR = nth root of (FV of +ve CF / PV of -ve CF) - 1.

Pros: Overcomes the reinvestment rate assumption of IRR, provides a single rate.

Cons: Requires the user to define a reinvestment rate, which can still be subjective.

Net Asset Value (NAV)


Definition: The total value of a fund's assets minus its liabilities, typically expressed on a per-share or per-unit basis.

Use Case: Fundamental for valuing investment funds (like mutual funds, ETFs, and private equity funds). It's used to calculate the value of an investor's stake in the fund.

Formula: NAV = (Total Assets - Total Liabilities) / No. of Outstanding Shares

Net Present Value (NPV)


Definition: The difference between the present value of cash inflows and the present value of cash outflows over a period of time.

Use Case: A primary metric for decision-making in financial analysis. If NPV is positive, the project is expected to be profitable; if negative, it's not. If zero, it breaks even.

Formula: NPV = E(CF / (1+r)^t) - Initial Investment.

Preference Shares (Preferred Stock)


Definition: A class of ownership in a corporation that has a higher claim on assets and earnings than common stock. They typically have fixed dividends, but usually do not carry voting rights.

Use Case: Common in venture capital and private equity deals as they offer downside protection (liquidation preference) and a fixed return for investors.

Pros: Priority in dividend payments and liquidation, fixed income stream, less volatile than common shares.

Cons: No voting rights, potential for lower capital appreciation compared to common stock if company grows significantly.

Convertible Notes


Definition: A type of short-term debt that converts into equity at a later date, typically upon a future equity financing round. They are often used by early-stage startups to defer valuation discussions.

Use Case: Popular in seed and early-stage fundraising for startups. They allow a company to raise capital quickly without setting a valuation, converting into equity when a larger, priced round occurs.

Pros: Simpler and quicker to execute than equity rounds, delays valuation discussion.

Cons: Can lead to complex cap table scenarios if not structured carefully, potential for investor dilution if future valuation is lower than expected.

Warrants


Definition: A long-term option to buy a company's stock at a predetermined price and date. They are often issued by companies as a "sweetener" for investors in debt or equity offerings.

Use Case: Commonly attached to private debt or preferred stock offerings in VC/PE deals to provide additional upside for investors. They give investors the right to purchase common shares at a set price.

Pros: Provides additional upside potential for investors, can make debt or preferred stock more attractive, less dilutive initially than direct equity.

Cons: Future dilution for existing shareholders if exercised, can complicate capital structure, value depends on future stock price performance.
